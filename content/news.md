# News

### 06-17-2025

We're excited to share our new paper on plant roots, just published in *Nature Communications*. For a long time, people thought plant roots mostly stayed near the surface and decay exponentially with depth. But by analyzing the NEON dataset which collected data from across North America, we found something different: many systems actually have a second peak of fine roots much deeper underground—often more than a meter down. This "bimodal" root pattern showed up at about 20% of the sites we studied, from Alaska to Puerto Rico.

These deep roots help plants reach water and nutrients that shallow roots can't get to. They also let plants store carbon deeper in the soil, where it's less likely to escape back into the air.

We also saw that these deep roots often form where there's extra nitrogen in the soil below the surface. Our results show that we might need to look deeper underground to really understand how plants work and how they affect the environment.

### 12-01-2024

Congratulations to [Shijun](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Ma-Shijun) for this new publication: [Carbon-negative transition by utilizing overlooked carbon in waste landfills](https://the-innovation.org/article/doi/10.59717/j.xinn-geo.2024.100109). In this paper, we explore an often-overlooked aspect of the global carbon cycle: carbon stored in modern city landfills. Landfills contribute to roughly one-tenth of global methane emissions, yet their standing carbon stock remains poorly characterized. In 346 Chinese cities alone, we estimate the landfill standing stock to be 0.5 Gigaton—equivalent to the weight of 50,000 Eiffel Towers or half the weight of all built structures in New York City, and about 1/20 of annual human carbon emissions. Studying different forms of urban waste can open new doors toward carbon neutrality. This research positions waste study as a novel frontier in global biogeochemistry.

### Press · 01-18-2024

In this news piece "[The new science of waste](https://www.santafe.edu/news-center/news/the-new-science-of-waste)," [Chris](https://www.santafe.edu/people/profile/chris-kempes) and I discuss the importance of not overlooking the by-product of our productive economy. Taking a complex systems approach, we treat these by-products as metabolic waste from urban systems and apply scaling theory to this unpleasant side of our society. We reveal amazing regularities that span hundreds to thousands of cities worldwide. Examining how cities generate waste is only the first step; we human society eventually needs to figure out a way to close the material loop. Natural ecosystems and the organisms within—from early multi-cellular life to the ancient wood-decaying fungi—have figured out how to deal with nature's "waste" for multi-million years; we can too. Check out our paper [here](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44284-023-00021-5).

Other news outlets that covered our work: [Phys.org](https://phys.org/news/2024-01-scaling-theory-approach-framework-pursue.html) · [ScienMag](https://scienmag.com/the-new-science-of-waste/) · [Wissenschaft.de](https://www.wissenschaft.de/erde-umwelt/was-die-abfallmenge-in-staedten-beeinflusst/) · [Techzle](https://techzle.com/what-affects-the-amount-of-waste-in-cities) · [WasteManagementReview](https://wastemanagementreview.com.au/research-looks-at-link-between-waste-production-and-city-size/) · [InsideWasteWeekly](https://www.insidewaste.com.au/89303-2-city-waste/) · [Envirotec](https://envirotecmagazine.com/2024/01/24/the-natural-world-should-inspire-a-new-science-of-waste/).
